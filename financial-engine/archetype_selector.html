Urban Micro-Mobility

Marketplace Model
E-scooter and e-bike sharing service launching in a mid-sized European city. Build the model for fleet deployment, unit economics, and multi-city expansion.
Asset-Heavy High Working Capital B2C

Business Overview

Concept: "VeloCity" - dock-less e-scooter and e-bike sharing service launching in Reykjavik, Iceland (population 130,000 + 2M annual tourists). Service via mobile app with per-minute pricing.

Value Proposition: Sustainable, convenient last-mile transportation for locals and tourists. Reduces car dependency in congested downtown areas.

Given Data & Benchmarks

  • Market: Reykjavik metro area 230,000 people, compact downtown, 8-month operating season (Apr-Nov)
  • Unit Economics: E-scooter purchase ‚Ç¨400, useful life 18 months, 3-5 rides/day typical
  • Pricing: Industry range ‚Ç¨1 unlock + ‚Ç¨0.15-0.30/min
  • Operating Costs: Repositioning/charging ‚Ç¨2-3/scooter/day, maintenance ‚Ç¨50/scooter/month
  • Competitor Benchmarks: Utilization rates 2-6 rides/scooter/day, average ride 15 minutes

Strategic Decisions You Must Make

  • Revenue Model: Pricing strategy? Per-minute only or subscription options? Tourist vs. local pricing?
  • Fleet Strategy: Initial fleet size? Growth plan? Mix of scooters vs. e-bikes?
  • Operations: In-house repositioning or contractor model? Charging infrastructure approach?
  • Expansion: When to expand to second city (Akureyri)? Growth vs. profitability tradeoff?
  • Capital Strategy: How much to raise? Asset financing vs. equity? Runway requirements?

Four Models Focus

  • Investment Model: Heavy capex for fleet, expansion capital needs
  • Revenue Model: Per-ride economics, seasonality impact, utilization drivers
  • Cost Model: Fixed (management, tech) vs. variable (repositioning, maintenance)
  • Cash Model: Upfront fleet purchase, daily cash collection, working capital light

Why This Archetype?

Teaches asset-intensive business modeling, seasonality, unit economics optimization, and the tension between growth and capital efficiency. Different from typical tech startups - helps understand physical asset businesses.

B2B SaaS Platform

Software Model
AI-powered sales enablement tool that integrates with CRMs to predict lead conversion likelihood. Classic SaaS economics with multiple go-to-market options.
Software Subscription Revenue B2B

Business Overview

Concept: "LeadScore AI" - SaaS platform that analyzes CRM data to predict which leads are most likely to convert, helping sales teams prioritize outreach and improve close rates.

Value Proposition: Increase sales productivity 15-30% by focusing reps on highest-potential leads. Reduces wasted effort on low-probability prospects.

Target Customer: B2B companies with 20-200 person sales teams (mid-market segment)

Given Data & Benchmarks

  • Market: TAM 50,000 companies in Europe, SAM 5,000 (realistic targets), SOM Year 1 target 50-150 customers
  • Typical SaaS Metrics: Gross margin 75-85%, Net dollar retention 110-130%, Monthly churn 2-4%
  • CAC Benchmarks: PLG motion ‚Ç¨3-5K per customer, Inside sales ‚Ç¨15-25K, Field sales ‚Ç¨50-80K
  • Cost Structure: Cloud infrastructure ‚Ç¨50-100/customer/month, Sales rep fully loaded ‚Ç¨150K/year (20 deals/year typical), CSM 1 per 50 customers
  • Pricing Range: Competitor range ‚Ç¨500-3000/month depending on seats and features

Strategic Decisions You Must Make

  • Revenue Model: Per-seat, per-lead-scored, or tiered packages? Target ACV ‚Ç¨25K, ‚Ç¨50K, or ‚Ç¨100K?
  • Go-to-Market: Product-led growth (PLG) + sales assist, pure inside sales, or field sales model?
  • Expansion Revenue: Land-and-expand strategy? Upsell paths? Multi-product vision?
  • Cost Structure: R&D investment level? How many sales reps Year 1? CAC payback target?
  • Capital Strategy: Seed round size? How long to Series A? Burn rate philosophy?

Four Models Focus

  • Investment Model: Capital needs driven by GTM strategy, timing of Series A
  • Revenue Model: MRR growth, churn dynamics, expansion revenue, pricing tiers
  • Cost Model: Gross margin (infrastructure), sales & marketing efficiency, R&D investment
  • Cash Model: Annual vs. monthly contracts (huge impact!), cash collection, burn rate

Why This Archetype?

SaaS is the most common VC-backed business model. Teaches cohort analysis, LTV:CAC ratios, the power of recurring revenue, and the cash impact of annual contracts. Multiple valid go-to-market strategies allow for strategic differentiation.

DTC Consumer Brand

Product Model
Sustainable athletic apparel brand selling performance wear made from recycled materials. Navigate inventory, channel strategy, and working capital challenges.
Product Inventory-Heavy B2C

Business Overview

Concept: "EcoFlow Athletic" - performance athletic apparel (running tights, sports bras, tops) made from recycled ocean plastics and other sustainable materials. Premium positioning targeting environmentally-conscious fitness enthusiasts.

Value Proposition: Performance-grade athletic wear with sustainability at its core. "Perform well, do good." Appeals to growing eco-conscious consumer segment willing to pay premium for sustainable products.

Given Data & Benchmarks

  • Market: UK athletic apparel market ¬£3.2B, sustainable segment growing 20% annually, target 0.1% share Year 3
  • Product Economics: Manufacturing COGS 30-35% of retail, sustainable materials add 10-15% premium
  • Pricing: Premium market - leggings ¬£75-95, sports bras ¬£45-60, tops ¬£40-55
  • Channels: DTC website (60-70% margin), wholesale retail (40-45% margin), own retail (55-60% margin)
  • Customer Acquisition: DTC CAC ¬£40-80 depending on channel (paid social, influencer, content)
  • Inventory: 90-day manufacturing lead time, 180-day payment terms from factory, seasonal collections

Strategic Decisions You Must Make

  • Revenue Model: Pure DTC or omnichannel? If wholesale, which retailers and when?
  • Product Strategy: Launch with full collection or start narrow? New product cadence?
  • Pricing: Super-premium (¬£100+ leggings) or accessible premium (¬£70-90)?
  • Customer Acquisition: Influencer-led, paid social, content marketing, or retail partnerships?
  • Inventory Management: Pre-order model? Made-to-order? Risk of overstock vs. stockouts?
  • Capital Needs: Working capital for inventory is biggest need - how much and when?

Four Models Focus

  • Investment Model: Working capital intensive! Inventory financing critical
  • Revenue Model: Channel mix (DTC vs. wholesale), repeat purchase rates, AOV optimization
  • Cost Model: COGS, contribution margin by channel, marketing efficiency
  • Cash Model: THIS IS THE CHALLENGE - inventory cycle, payment terms, seasonality cash crunch

Why This Archetype?

Teaches working capital management challenges that plague physical product businesses. The cash model really matters here - you can be profitable on paper but run out of cash due to inventory timing. Great for understanding the difference between profit and cash flow.

Local Service Chain

Service Model
Premium mobile pet grooming service expanding from one successful location to a regional chain. Build the replication model and franchise vs. corporate trade-offs.
Service Labor-Intensive B2C

Business Overview

Concept: "Pampered Paws Mobile Grooming" - upscale mobile pet grooming service. Fully equipped vans come to customer's home. Current single-van operation in Bath profitable; founder wants to expand to 10+ vans across Southwest England.

Value Proposition: Stress-free grooming experience for pets (no travel, familiar environment), convenient for owners (no time lost dropping off), premium service quality. Price premium 20-30% vs. traditional groomers.

Given Data & Benchmarks

  • Market: Bath-Bristol corridor 600K population, 35% dog ownership, ¬£60-80 average grooming visit
  • Unit Economics: Van + equipment ¬£45K, groomer salary ¬£28-35K/year, fuel/maintenance ¬£400/month
  • Capacity: 4-6 appointments/day, ¬£70 average ticket, 5-day work week, 80% capacity typical
  • Customer Dynamics: Grooming frequency 6-8 weeks, 60-70% repeat rate, word-of-mouth strong
  • Operating Model Options: Owner-operator vans, employed groomers, franchise model (¬£25K franchise fee + 6% royalty)

Strategic Decisions You Must Make

  • Expansion Model: Company-owned or franchise? Mixed model? What's optimal?
  • Growth Pace: Add 2 vans/year or 5 vans/year? Geographic clustering vs. spread?
  • Operating Model: Employed groomers (control) or independent contractors (capital efficiency)?
  • Pricing Strategy: Maintain premium or move toward volume?
  • Marketing: Local marketing per van or corporate brand building? CAC strategy?
  • Capital Needs: How much for expansion? Debt vs. equity? Franchise fees as capital source?

Four Models Focus

  • Investment Model: Capex per van, franchise model changes capital needs dramatically
  • Revenue Model: Revenue per van, capacity utilization, ticket price optimization
  • Cost Model: Fixed (corporate overhead) vs. variable (groomer, fuel), operating leverage as you scale
  • Cash Model: Immediate cash collection (services), low working capital, franchise fees boost cash if chosen

Why This Archetype?

Service businesses have different economics than product or software. Labor is the main cost, capacity utilization is key, and the franchise vs. corporate decision fundamentally changes the financial engine. Great for understanding operating leverage and replication models.

Healthcare Tech Platform

Hybrid Model
IoT remote patient monitoring platform for chronic disease management. Complex model combining hardware, software, and healthcare reimbursement dynamics.
Hardware + Software B2B2C Regulated

Business Overview

Concept: "VitalConnect" - IoT devices (wearable sensors) + cloud platform that monitors chronic disease patients (diabetes, hypertension, heart failure) at home. Alerts care teams to concerning trends before hospitalization needed.

Value Proposition: Reduce hospital readmissions 25-40%, improve patient outcomes, lower healthcare costs. Revenue from NHS/insurance reimbursement + subscription platform fees to healthcare providers.

Given Data & Benchmarks

  • Market: UK 15M chronic disease patients, targeting 200K patients by Year 5 start
  • Device Economics: Device COGS ¬£120, subsidized to patient ¬£50-200, 18-month device life
  • Revenue Model: NHS reimbursement ¬£40-60/patient/month, Platform subscription ¬£20/patient/month to provider
  • Clinical Validation: Must demonstrate outcomes in pilot (6-12 months), affects reimbursement approval
  • Sales Cycle: NHS procurement 9-12 months, phased rollout typical (pilot ‚Üí full contract)
  • Operating Costs: Clinical support team ¬£45K/year per 1000 patients, tech platform ¬£5/patient/month

Strategic Decisions You Must Make

  • Business Model: B2B (sell to NHS/insurers) or B2B2C (direct to patients)? Hybrid?
  • Device Strategy: Subsidize heavily for patient adoption or charge full price? Impact on working capital?
  • Clinical Validation: How much to invest in pilots? Delay revenue but strengthen reimbursement case?
  • Geographic Expansion: NHS first or diversify to private insurance markets? Reimbursement varies hugely by country
  • R&D Investment: How fast to develop multi-condition platform vs. single-condition depth?
  • Capital Needs: Long sales cycles + device subsidy = high cash needs. How much and when?

Four Models Focus

  • Investment Model: Capital-intensive (device subsidy + long sales cycles), clinical validation costs
  • Revenue Model: Recurring (reimbursement + subscription) but with device sale component, complex revenue recognition
  • Cost Model: Device COGS + clinical support + tech platform, economies of scale emerge slowly
  • Cash Model: Device subsidy upfront, reimbursement monthly with lag, working capital challenge

Why This Archetype?

Most complex of the five archetypes - combines hardware, software, regulated market, long sales cycles, and reimbursement dynamics. Great for teams who want a challenge and to understand how capital-intensive, complex models differ from typical tech startups. Teaches patience and milestone planning.

Frequently Asked Questions

What if we have our own venture idea we're passionate about?

We encourage you to work with the provided archetypes for this assignment. They're designed to be equally modelable and fair for assessment. However, if you have an exceptional idea that's equally modelable, you may submit it for approval. The bar is high - it must have clear unit economics and all four models must be readily constructible.

How do we divide work across our group?

Each group member can "own" one of the four models, but everyone must understand how they interconnect. For Milestone 1, work together on the blueprint. For Milestone 2, divide model construction but integrate at the end. The assumptions document should reflect collective thinking.

What's more important - accuracy or thoughtful assumptions?

Thoughtful assumptions. We're not expecting perfect forecasts - these are startups, uncertainty is inherent. What matters is: (1) Can you articulate WHY you assumed something? (2) Have you identified which assumptions are most critical? (3) Have you shown sensitivity to key assumptions? (4) Do your four models fit together coherently?

What if multiple groups choose the same archetype?

That's expected and actually valuable! Seeing two groups model the same business differently is a great learning opportunity. It shows there's no single "right" answer - strategic choices matter. We'll aim for 1-2 groups per archetype for comparison purposes.
